Family Offices Are Increasingly Going Direct on Investments

👤 Victoria Blackwell 📅 February 26, 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read

The family office sector has undergone a quiet revolution over the past decade. What were once primarily allocation vehicles—distributing capital across external fund managers—have increasingly become active investors in their own right. Family offices managing assets for wealthy dynasties are building internal investment teams, developing proprietary deal flow, and taking direct stakes in private companies that bypass traditional private equity structures entirely. This shift is reshaping competitive dynamics in private markets and creating new opportunities for entrepreneurs seeking patient capital.

The numbers reflect the trend's magnitude. Industry surveys indicate that direct investments now represent 30-40% of the average family office portfolio, up from less than 20% a decade ago. The largest family offices have established investment operations that rival mid-sized private equity firms in sophistication, with dedicated teams for real estate, venture capital, growth equity, and operating businesses. Some have accumulated sufficient scale and expertise to lead transactions that would previously have required institutional sponsors.

Several factors are driving the move toward direct investing. Most prominently, fee savings can be substantial. Traditional fund structures charge management fees of 1.5-2% annually plus carried interest of 20% on profits—costs that compound significantly over long holding periods. Family offices making direct investments avoid these fees, retaining more of the investment returns for beneficiaries. For a $1 billion portfolio with typical private equity allocations, the lifetime fee savings from direct investing can exceed $100 million.

Beyond economics, direct investing offers strategic advantages that align with family office investment philosophies. Family offices often seek investments they can hold indefinitely, without the pressure to exit that private equity funds face as they approach the end of their fund lives. They can be more patient with companies that need time to develop, and more flexible in structuring transactions that accommodate founder preferences. For entrepreneurs, family office capital has become an increasingly attractive alternative to traditional VC and PE funding.

Co-investment alongside institutional fund managers represents a middle ground that many family offices pursue. In these arrangements, family offices invest directly in specific deals sourced by private equity sponsors, often on a fee-free or reduced-fee basis. Co-investment allows families to benefit from institutional due diligence and deal-making capabilities while avoiding the full fee load of fund commitments. Competition for co-investment allocations has intensified as more families recognize the attractive economics.

The professionalization of family office investment operations has accelerated rapidly. Family offices are recruiting talent from top private equity firms, investment banks, and corporate development teams. They're building data infrastructure and analytical capabilities that enable sophisticated deal evaluation. Some have established formal investment processes with dedicated committees, while others maintain the flexibility and speed that has always been a family office advantage. The best operations combine institutional rigor with the agility and long-term perspective that set family capital apart.

Challenges accompany the direct investing trend. Building and maintaining a high-quality deal pipeline requires sustained effort and network development. Evaluating complex transactions demands expertise that smaller family offices may lack. And direct investments are inherently illiquid, creating portfolio management challenges that fund investments handle more easily. Family offices that pursue direct strategies must realistically assess their capabilities and resources before committing to approaches that require institutional-level execution.
