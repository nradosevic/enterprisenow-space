Japan's Economic Resurgence: After 30 Years, What's Changed

👤 Kenji Watanabe 📅 March 8, 2026 ⏱️ 10 min read

The Nikkei 225's breach of its 1989 all-time high in early 2024 marked a symbolic milestone that captured global attention, but the more substantive story lies in the structural transformations that have revitalized Japan as an investment destination. After three decades characterized by deflation, aging demographics, and corporate stagnation, Japan has emerged as one of the most compelling equity markets for international investors. Understanding what has changed—and what risks remain—is essential for investors considering Japanese allocations.

Corporate governance reform stands as perhaps the most significant driver of Japan's equity market renaissance. The introduction of Japan's Stewardship Code in 2014 and Corporate Governance Code in 2015 initiated a fundamental shift in the relationship between companies and shareholders. Cross-shareholdings, once pervasive and value-destructive, have declined substantially as companies have unwound positions under investor pressure. Board composition has evolved, with independent directors now comprising majorities at most large companies. Return on equity, long depressed by inefficient capital allocation, has improved meaningfully across the market.

The Tokyo Stock Exchange's 2023 initiative requiring companies trading below book value to disclose improvement plans accelerated these trends. Over 40% of companies in the TOPIX index traded below book value at the initiative's launch, a striking indication of accumulated corporate inefficiency. Companies have responded with share buybacks, dividend increases, and strategic divestitures of non-core assets. The aggregate buyback activity in Japanese equities has reached record levels, with companies returning capital that was previously locked in low-yielding cash balances and strategic investments with questionable rationale.

Monetary policy normalization represents another significant development, though one that carries execution risks. The Bank of Japan's gradual exit from negative interest rate policy and yield curve control marks the end of an extraordinary era of monetary accommodation. While higher rates have historically been viewed as headwinds for equity markets, in Japan's context the normalization reflects underlying economic health—sustained inflation, wage growth, and improved corporate pricing power. The yen's stabilization following years of depreciation has made Japanese assets more attractive to unhedged foreign investors.

Japan's position in global supply chains has strengthened amid geopolitical tensions and supply chain restructuring. The "friend-shoring" trend, in which Western companies seek to reduce dependence on Chinese manufacturing, has benefited Japanese suppliers in semiconductors, industrial equipment, and precision manufacturing. Japan's technological capabilities in areas including robotics, advanced materials, and automotive components remain world-leading. The government's strategic investments in semiconductor manufacturing capacity, including substantial subsidies for TSMC's Japanese facilities, reflect a coordinated effort to capitalize on these trends.

Warren Buffett's well-publicized investments in Japan's trading houses—Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo, Itochu, and Marubeni—provided a powerful endorsement that attracted additional institutional attention. These trading companies, with their diversified business models and exposure to commodities and global trade, have delivered exceptional returns as energy prices rose and supply chains restructured. The Buffett investments demonstrated that even the most value-focused investors could find compelling opportunities in a market long dismissed as a value trap.

Significant risks and challenges remain, however. Japan's demographic trajectory—with a population that is both shrinking and aging rapidly—creates structural headwinds for domestic consumption and labor force growth. Productivity improvements and automation can partially offset these trends, but the math remains challenging. Geopolitical risks, particularly related to regional security concerns involving China, Taiwan, and North Korea, create latent vulnerabilities. Currency volatility remains elevated, and the yen's trajectory will significantly influence returns for international investors. Despite the progress achieved, Japanese equity valuations have expanded considerably, reducing the margin of safety that attracted value investors initially.
