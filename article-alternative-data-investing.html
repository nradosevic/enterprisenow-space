Alternative Data in Investing: Satellite Imagery, Credit Card Data, and the Edge Game

👤 Dr. Samuel Okonkwo 📅 March 1, 2026 ⏱️ 10 min read

The institutionalization of alternative data has transformed how sophisticated investors approach fundamental research and alpha generation. What began as an edge for a handful of pioneering quantitative funds has evolved into a mainstream practice employed across the investment management industry. Satellite imagery tracking retail parking lots, credit card transaction panels measuring consumer spending, web scraping capturing pricing changes, and sentiment analysis derived from social media all now inform investment decisions at scale. Understanding the current landscape—including both the opportunities and limitations of alternative data—is essential for investors seeking to compete in increasingly efficient markets.

The fundamental premise of alternative data is simple: traditional sources of investment information, including company filings, earnings calls, and sell-side research, are simultaneously available to all market participants. Any informational advantage derived from these sources is quickly arbitraged away. Alternative data offers the potential for exclusive or early access to insights that can predict fundamental performance before it appears in traditional disclosures. A hedge fund that knows same-store sales trends two weeks before quarterly earnings has a meaningful advantage over investors relying solely on company guidance and consensus estimates.

Satellite imagery has become one of the most established alternative data categories. Companies like Orbital Insight and SpaceKnow process imagery from commercial satellites to track activity at retail locations, industrial facilities, and shipping ports. Parking lot car counts at major retailers have shown strong correlations with quarterly revenue performance. Oil storage tank measurements enable crude inventory estimates independent of government statistics. Agricultural yield estimates derived from crop imagery inform commodity trading strategies. The technology has matured to the point where imagery updates are available daily or even more frequently for many locations.

Credit card and transaction data provides direct insight into consumer behavior, though the category involves significant complexity. Data providers aggregate transaction records from panels of consumers, anonymizing individual-level information while preserving spending pattern insights. The analytical challenge lies in extrapolating from panel data to company-level estimates, accounting for sample biases, seasonal adjustments, and competitive dynamics. Leading practitioners combine transaction data with other sources, including web traffic, app download data, and pricing information, to build more robust revenue models.

The proliferation of alternative data has raised important questions about market fairness and the sustainability of informational edges. As more investors adopt similar data sources and analytical techniques, the value of any individual insight diminishes. The alternative data industry has responded with increasingly specialized and proprietary datasets, creating an arms race in which the cost of maintaining competitive advantage continues to rise. Smaller investors without resources to license multiple data feeds and employ dedicated data science teams face structural disadvantages that may be difficult to overcome.

Regulatory and legal considerations add further complexity. The SEC has scrutinized certain alternative data practices, particularly those involving "web scraping" of company websites or data obtained in violation of contractual terms. The use of geolocation data from mobile devices has attracted privacy concerns and potential regulatory action. Investment managers must establish robust compliance frameworks to ensure that alternative data acquisition and usage do not create legal or reputational risks. Due diligence on data providers has become as important as due diligence on the investments themselves.

For individual investors and smaller institutions, the implications of the alternative data revolution are sobering. The playing field has become decidedly unequal in terms of informational resources available to different market participants. However, some observers argue that individual investors can still succeed by focusing on areas where alternative data advantages are less pronounced, such as smaller capitalization stocks with limited institutional coverage, longer time horizons where short-term data signals lose relevance, or qualitative judgments about management quality and competitive positioning that resist quantification. The edge may have shifted, but it has not disappeared entirely.
