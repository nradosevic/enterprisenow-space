Why CEO Succession Planning Still Fails at Most Companies

👤 Patricia Morrison 📅 February 18, 2026 ⏱️ 7 min read

The boardroom presents a paradox. Directors universally acknowledge that CEO succession planning ranks among their most critical responsibilities. Consultants have built lucrative practices around leadership development and transition management. Yet despite decades of professional attention, botched CEO successions remain remarkably common—destroying shareholder value, derailing strategic initiatives, and sometimes precipitating organizational crises. Understanding why succession planning continues to fail requires examining the structural and psychological barriers that persist even in well-governed companies.

The fundamental challenge is temporal mismatch. Effective succession planning requires sustained attention over years—identifying potential successors, providing developmental experiences, evaluating performance in varied contexts, and building relationships that enable smooth transitions. Yet board time is finite and typically dominated by immediate business issues. The CEO succession discussion, nominally scheduled for annual review, often gets compressed or deferred as more urgent matters claim attention. The result is chronic underinvestment in a process that matters enormously but rarely feels urgent until it's too late.

Incumbent CEOs complicate the dynamics in subtle ways. Even leaders who intellectually embrace succession planning may unconsciously resist actions that highlight their own replaceability. They may delay designating clear successors, maintain ambiguity about retirement timing, or subtly undermine internal candidates who might seem ready to step up. These behaviors rarely reflect malice—more often, they stem from the deeply human desire to remain relevant and valuable. Boards that fail to recognize and manage these dynamics allow succession planning to become captive to incumbent psychology.

The internal versus external candidate decision presents another frequent failure point. Research suggests that internal successors outperform external hires on average, benefiting from institutional knowledge and established relationships. Yet boards often become enamored with the perceived transformational potential of outside leaders, particularly when the company faces strategic challenges. The grass-is-greener phenomenon leads to external appointments that fail when new leaders cannot navigate unfamiliar cultures and stakeholder relationships. Conversely, overly insular boards may overlook the genuine need for fresh perspectives that outsiders can provide.

Evaluation criteria for successor candidates often miss the mark. Boards tend to weight heavily the qualities that made previous CEOs successful, which may or may not match future requirements. The executive who excelled at operational efficiency may not be equipped for a digital transformation. The leader who built the company's current strategy may struggle to recognize when that strategy needs fundamental revision. More sophisticated boards are learning to define future-oriented competency models, but the tendency to fight the last war remains strong.

The transition process itself deserves more attention than it typically receives. Even when the right successor is identified, handoff execution matters enormously. Transitions that are too abrupt leave new CEOs without institutional support; those that are too gradual create confusion about authority and decision-making. Managing the relationship between outgoing and incoming leaders—particularly when both remain with the organization—requires diplomatic skill that not all boards possess. The liminal period around leadership transitions is when organizational vulnerabilities are most acute.

What distinguishes companies that manage succession well? They begin the process early—years before any transition is anticipated. They invest in developing multiple potential successors, creating genuine optionality rather than coronating a single candidate prematurely. They maintain clear separation between succession planning and the incumbent CEO's performance evaluation. And they recognize that succession is not a one-time event but an ongoing board responsibility that requires consistent attention regardless of the current CEO's tenure. The companies that take these steps are more likely to navigate leadership transitions smoothly—and to compound value across leadership generations rather than experiencing the disruption that poorly managed successions inevitably cause.
